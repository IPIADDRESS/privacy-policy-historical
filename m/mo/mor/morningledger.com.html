Our Commitment To Privacy

How Do We Use Information We Collect from Cookies?

We use Web site browser software tools such as cookies and Web server logs to gather information about our Web site users’ browsing activities, in order to constantly improve our Web site and better serve our customers. This information assists us to design and arrange our Web pages in the most user-friendly manner and to continually improve our Web site to better meet the needs of our customers and prospective customers. Web server logs allow us to count how many people visit our Web site and evaluate our Web site’s visitor capacity. We do not use these technologies to capture your individual email address or any personally identifying information about you although they do permit us to send focused online banner advertisements or other such responses to you.

We do not share your information with outside parties. We use the information you provide about someone else when placing an request only for the tell-a-friend contest. Such addresses are not used for any other purpose and are not shared with outside parties. Finally, we never use or share the personally identifiable information provided to us online in ways unrelated to the ones described above without also providing you an opportunity to opt-out or otherwise prohibit such unrelated uses.

Our Commitment To Data Security

    Apple’s big event for developers, WWDC 2015 will soon kick off and like always, fans are expecting some new upgrades and developments from the company at the event. WWDC has never been the platform to showcase new products, so expect it to be focused on the updates to the company’s softwares like iOS and OS X operating systems.

    According to analysts, Apple may introduce one new product at the event, a brand new Apple TV. Its been a while since the product got a significant update and WWDC could be a good place to unveil the new TV. This could be supported by a new Apple TV Remote as well with features such as voice commands. However, even if the company doesn’t announce a new TV box, the company could release a new update which can change the overall UI in existing Apple Tvs. This could bring new features such as live streaming, TV subscription service etc.

    One of the surest announcement at the event will be the updates to the iOS and OS X operating systems. Its almost time to see the new iOS update, the iOS 9, so expect the announcement soon. However, analysts are of the opinion that iOS 9 may not bring any significant design changes as the iOS 7, but rather it will be more focused on boosting performance and fixing bugs. The same goes with OS 11 which will also debut at the event.

    Some of the possible feature additions could be the rumored Home app, which will be a dedicated application to manage all of the user’s homekit enabled devices. At present, these devices work with Siri with voice activation, however, it is said that a dedicated app will debut at the event for simplifying things further. Even Siri is expected to receive a significant upgrade to help it better compete with rivals such as Google Now. As for the new OS X, it is said that users could get a control center access from the left edge of the screen for quick access to important features.

    If rumors are to be believed, then Apple will unveil a paid music streaming service too at the event, alongside a free version. The paid service (rumored to cost $10) will compete with the likes of spotify and Google Play Music.

    Another important announcement at WWDC will most likely be the native SDK for Apple Watch which will let developers create apps that run directly on the Apple Wearable instead of the connected iPhone.

    Apple’s WWDC 2015 kicks off on Monday at Moscone Center in San Francisco.

    Microsoft has finally revealed the launch date of the upcoming Windows 10 OS. The company’s Vice President of Operating Systems, Terry Myerson, said in a blog post earlier today that the new Windows 10 OS will be released on June 29. The company had earlier said that it would release the new OS by summer of 2015.

    The upgrade will be free for most users who are running the existing Windows 8 OS and the previous generation Windows 7 OS. However, the company is yet to reveal a price tag for those users who are ineligible for the upgrade. Also, there’s still no word on the launch date of the mobile version of the OS.

    Microsoft has reportedly made many changes and additions to this new version of Windows. The OS is now streamlined for both home use as well as enterprise use. Unlike earlier editions, Windows 10 will run on a wide number of devices including desktops, laptops, smartphones, and tablets. It will also run on various IoT (Internet of thing) devices as well as work on large servers.

    The basic idea is to bring everything under one OS. So, if a person starts a spreadsheet on his laptop, Windows 10 would allow him to carry on with his work when he switches to a mobile device like a smartphone. The OS is built to look and feel the same regardless of the size, which is why it will even work on virtual reality headsets. However, on July 29, only the desktop version of the OS will be unveiled.

    Although the new OS will come with a lot of new features, Microsoft is particular highlighting a couple of those like the new personal assistant Cortana as well as a redesigned web browser as Edge. This will be the first time Cortana will be making it to the desktop versions of Windows. The personal assistant can reportedly perform actions such as answering questions, taking down notes etc. Unfortunately, Cortana will be available only in US, UK, China, Italy, France, Germany, and Spain at the time of launch.

    Windows Hello, the biometric authentication service will also be available to desktops and laptops which feature fingerprint recognition or an infrared camera for facial recognition. The revamped Edge browser aims to improve the overall web browsing experience of the user with a simple design. The Xbox app will also make it to the desktop versions of the OS.

    While dinosaurs have long captured the imagination of people everywhere – so much about their origins remains a mystery. Long has it been argued whether they were warm-blooded or cold-blooded. While plenty of evidence has appeared to support the former, perhaps the question is the wrong one to ask.  We now know that these types are not neat divisions – with some animals such as modern crocodiles being able to routinely switch between being warm and cold-blooded, while enjoying the advantages of both.

    Last year, the University of New Mexico biologist John Grady and his colleagues published their new in-depth analysis of dinosaur physiology by studying the growth rates from fossil records. The study concluded that dinosaurs may have been a type of animal known as a mesotherm – which means that they were capable of regulating their bodies at a higher temperature than their habitat, but that there was no set point to it, meaning that there was no calorie limit set for them to maintain a set temperature.

    Not everyone bought it. In the new issue of Science, both Stony Brook University paleontologist Mike D’Emic and Nathan Myhrvold refuted the idea in two separate stories.

    Myhrvold’s maintains that when working, Grady and colleagues “deviated from accepted statistical practice” as they estimated the growth rate, therefore formulating an “illusion” of mesothermic dinosaurs. Myhrvold concludes “growth rate does not predict metabolism.”

    D’Emic found another critical flaw in the study – while dinosaur lifespans were determined by rating yearly growth spans of bone, it is likely that they grew at different rates during different seasons, while the study also fails to account for time differences in days and years in the Mesozoic Era.

    What D’Emic found when he doubled the rates, to account for longer periods of growth, was rates indicative of endothermic metabolisms – similar to placental mammals. However, this does not necessarily indicate that all of them were hot-blooded, just that this would be a more accurate calculation based on Grady’s accumulation of data. The findings of the Dinosaur Renaissance still remain as invaluable to paleontology as ever – whether dinosaurs were agile and intelligent is no longer in dispute, but the debate over what physiology allowed them to thrive continues to be a strongly debated mystery.

    Researchers have found jaw bones and teeth from a prehistoric human ancestor that lived between 3.3 and 3.5 million years ago, the same time when several other early human species, roamed the Earth, and spreading the size of our family tree. The new study has been published in the journal Nature.

    Australopithecus deyiremeda is the name for this new ancestor, borrowing a word meaning “close relative” to the indigenous Afar people of Ethiopia, where the bones were found.  The new species was closely related to Lucy who lived about a half million years later.

    Dr. Yohannes Haile-Selassie, team leader and the curator of physical anthropology at the Cleveland Museum of Natural History in the US, told BBC News: “We had to look at the detailed anatomy and morphology of the teeth and the upper and lower jaws, and we found major differences.

    “This new species has very robust jaws. In addition, we see this new species had smaller teeth. The canine is really small – smaller than all known hominins we have documented in the past.”

    There remains some dispute over whether or not these remains in fact constitute members of different species, as they closely resemble some remains found in Kenya in 2001, suggesting that Lucy, commonly thought of as a direct ancestor, came from quite a sophisticated line. According to Dr. Haile-Selassie, said the early stage of human evolution was probably surprisingly complex.

    “Historically, because we didn’t have the fossil evidence to show there was hominin diversity during the middle Pliocene, we thought there was only one lineage, one primitive ancestor – in this case Australopithecus afarensis, Lucy – giving rise to the next.

    “That hypothesis of linear evolution has to be revisited. And now with the discovery of more species, like this new one… you have another species roaming around.

    “What this means is we have many species that could give rise to later hominins, including our own genus Homo.”

    However, he wishes to continue the hunt, saying that many more fossils need to be unearthed in order to advance our own understanding of human evolution. Of particular interest is any evidence that might support the notion that different species of hominins lived next to each other.

    A Bronze Age girl who died in Denmark some 3,400 years ago may have been well traveled, according to a new analysis of her hair and teeth, perhaps putting to rest the thought that ancient people rarely traveled far from the lands where they lived and died.

    The Egtved girl, named for the village where here remains were discovered, was first dug up in 1921. While all bones were missing from her remains, the clothing, hair, nails and some teeth remained in pristine condition.

    The new analysis, published May 21 in Scientific Reports, has shown that she may have traveled there from southern Germany, one of several long trips in her final two years. The remains are currently being held at the National Museum of Denmark.

    She was between 16 and 18 years old and found in a peat bog, sharing an oak coffin with the cremated remains of a five to six year old child.

    The acid in the peat created a thin layer of iron over the coffin. Rainwater entered, but couldn’t get out – causing the bones to decay, but the lack of oxygen preserved her hair, nails and clothing.

    She was buried in a wool skirt belted with a large bronze disk containing spirals on it.

    “She looks, in a way, very modern, in this kind of miniskirt and a kind of T-shirt,” said archaeologist Karin Frei in an interview with Live Science. (Her unique style has already inspired a number of re-enactors on Pinterest.)

    Figurines dating to the same depict women in similar clothing, wearing spirals connected to a Scandinavian sun cult, so historians have concluded the girl must have been a priestess of that cult, Frei said.

    Analyzing traces of strontium isotopes in the wool skirt, Frei was able to indicate where she lived. The wool itself, however, was a big indicator, as it was likely from the Black Forest of Germany, quite a distance from Denmark. Hair samples also revealed how often she grew and cut her hair over her last two years of life.

    “She moved from one place outside Denmark, to a place that could be Denmark, to a place very far from Denmark,” where she spent a large portion of the last six months of her life,” Frei said. “She probably died or got sick and died very shortly after her arrival to Egtved,” Frei said.
