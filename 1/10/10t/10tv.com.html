  • Best Bike Blog Ever

    A Christmas bike miracle

    Enough about me already ... here's a feel-good holiday bike story...

    Sue Olander stared and stared in disbelief at the spot where her beloved bicycle, which she called Attitude Adjuster, should have been.

    It just had to be there.

    But her Trek Madone was really and completely gone, stolen by a dastardly bicycle thief. 

    “I was shaking and I couldn’t think; I thought I would throw up,” Sue said. “My world started spinning on me; I couldn’t get my phone out of my back pocket to call my husband to tell him someone had stolen my bike.”

    It was October 20, and Sue had just finished riding the Columbus Fall City Ride and was at North Market with 30 or so other cyclists, many from the Westerville Bicycle Club, of which Sue is a member.

    They’d locked up their bikes, gone inside to grab something to eat, and came back out, ready for the ride home.

    Also stolen was the bike of Gina Smith, Sue’s friend. They had been locked together, with a single lock.

    “Someone cut through it,” Sue said. “They left the lock there, and one of my gloves. They took my helmet and mirror and one of my gloves. They took Gina’s helmet and her seat bag.”

    Gina’s seat bag had the keys to her car and apartment inside, adding to her problems.

    In most cases, that would have been that … the bikes would have been lost forever, eventually sold via the internet to someone with no idea they were buying stolen merchandise.

    But Sue was determined to get Attitude Adjuster back and solicited the help of her friends.

    “I had a team of people trying to find it,” she said of the daily cyber search she and her husband, Gary, conducted, along with Gina, Larry Graham, Craig Clark, Hans Monning, Jodi Dzuranin and Joanna Pallos. They were a band of highly motivated, cyberspace bike detectives.

    “I had nightmares and anxiety attacks about losing my bicycle,” said Sue, who works for the state, setting up vaccines for children without health insurance. She commutes daily by bike from her home in North Columbus to her Downtown office.

    Sue and Gary had lovingly upgraded her Trek with several hard-to-find components and she rode Attitude Adjuster almost every day, 3,800 miles in 2012 and about 2,200 this year.

    And when she rode her Trek, her mood was instantly better, hence the Attitude Adjuster nickname. Her Trek was more than a collection of carbon and components, “it’s like it’s your baby,” Sue said.

    Her miles were down this year due to some medical issues.

    “I was at my mom’s for Christmas and their dog bit off half my nose,” Sue said of the December 2012 incident that required several subsequent procedures and surgeries to correct.

    “It’s been a rough year,” she said. “I’ve had six surgeries since January 2.”

    More than ever, she needed Attitude Adjuster. And now her bike was gone.

    And then, one day, she got a text from Larry.

    “He said I think I found your bicycle,” Sue said.

    Larry had found it on Craigslist. Sue checked the description and photo on the website and, sure enough, it was Attitude Adjuster.

    “Gary said to call the police,” said Sue, who did just that. “But I got the runaround, they gave me two numbers to call and I left messages but didn’t hear back.”

    Sue asked Gary to call the police and give it a try, which he did, and his persistence paid off. The police were on the case, contacted the bike seller and set up a meeting.

    “The guy (who had her bike) said he wasn’t comfortable having people come to his house, so they met him at the parking lot of the Steak ‘n Shake on Polaris Parkway,” Sue said. “There were two cops going, to make the fake buy.”

    Sue doesn’t know the details of how the bust went down or the subsequent criminal charges, but the police got Attitude Adjuster back and were also able to recover Gina’s bike when they eventually went to the seller’s house.

    “ He said he got them from his mechanic,” said Sue, who doesn’t buy the story one bit. “I don’t know if he was released or went to jail, but he was in police custody.”

    Sue got her bike back on Dec. 18.

    “I was jumping up and down and clapping and crying and was shaking,” said Sue, who thinks of it as her Christmas miracle. “It’s an amazingly happy ending.”

    She and Larry (L) Gary (in the photo with Attitude Adjuster) and their bikes went on a celebratory ride on Sunday, as the rain stopped for a few hours and it was sort of warm out.

    Sue also has some advice for others who have had their bikes stolen, an occurrence that is unfortunately all too common.

    Write down the registration number and take photos of your bike, especially things that are distinctive to your particular bike.

    “And keep checking Craigslist and get a team of people you know and trust to ask for help finding it,” Sue said.
