Editor's Take
Outside the envelope
June 11, 2009

The crash of an Airbus A330 several hundreds miles northeast of Brazil last week was the worst aviation disaster in the history of Air France, and also the worst since 2001. Unlike many deadly accidents, which seem to result from faulty engines, pilot error or horrendous weather, the loss of Flight 447 seems to be tied to the failure of something far smaller: the pitot tube.

Named for its French inventor, the device operates on a fluids principle described by Daniel Bernoulli. The pitot tube captures air from the front of the aircraft, slowing it to “stagnation” because the tube has no outlet. The pressure at the stagnation point can be measured. To obtain the airspeed, this measurement must be differentiation from the static pressure, which is easily obtained by ports at other oblique points on the aircraft or by a concentric tube built into the pitot tube assembly.

Perfected in the 19th century by Henry Darcy, long before air travel, there’s little to go wrong with a pitot tube. If kept clean and free of debris, they are a dependable source of information for an aircraft’s flight computer. They are even used on F1 race cars.

Unfortunately these tubes, which often quite small can easily get clogged. Usually flagged and covered while on tarmac, as these tubes are exposed to another danger in the air: ice.

Frozen pitot tubes have been tied to other air disasters, but verifying a frozen tube post-crash is difficult. The possibility makes sense in those cases where the onboard computers suddenly spit out a lot of contradictory information. Air movement is entirely a difference in pressure. If the static ports on a plane are clogged or frozen, pilots have few useful readings from their instruments.

Of course, other possibilities loom. Lightning can easily wreak havoc. Wings can easily ice up, altering the aerodynamic profile to dangerous levels. But the pitot tube failure scenario is entirely plausible. Using inaccurate airspeed and altitude data, the pilots may have adjusted throttle and trim to the point of stress failure. Buffeted by stormy winds, the failure of an aileron might have spelled disaster. Sudden loss of control would explain the sudden disappearance of all communication.

With the help of sophisticated software and a nuclear-powered hunter-killer submarine, the French have a slim chance of recovering the black boxes that will tell the truth. For now, the guessing will have to continue.
