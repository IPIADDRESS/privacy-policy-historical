Editor's Take
Japanese billboard watches you
Dec. 17, 2008

In January, NTT Communications will begin a technology test that’s right out of science fiction: a billboard that looks back at you. At a Tokyo railway station, a flat panel display advertising various products will have a small camera attached above it, connected to image analysis software. The camera will scan travelers to see how many of them are looking at the display. The image detection software uses the characteristics of “an average Japanese face,” and, by using pattern matching, recognizes faces in the image. A second camera will estimate the number of people in front of the ad and whether or not they are looking at it.

The system doesn’t go quite as far as Minority Report’s personalized ads, as it doesn’t identify the individuals in the images, it merely detects and counts them. NTT is aware of the negative implications of such a system and is using the technology with its content distribution system for digital signs to gauge the effectiveness of digital advertising. If this technology proves useful, however, it’s a small jump from counting the individuals in an image to identifying them. We all know about the prevalence of security cameras, but the use of advanced imaging to specifically learn about our preferences with or without our consent is disconcerting. The value placed on e-mail lists is high—we can imagine how valuable these images might be, especially if additional software is used to identify individuals. Misuse or theft, already a pitfall for Internet users, could follow. Let’s just hope such privacy invasions stay relegated to the sci fi realm and not reality.
